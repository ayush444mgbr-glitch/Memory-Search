**Diary Entry – Ravish**
**Date:** 12 October

Today might become one of the most important days of my life.

I attended a tech conference today, mostly expecting the usual things—talks about AI, startups, and the future of technology. But something unexpected happened. I got a chance to meet **Sam Altman**, the CEO of OpenAI. I still can’t believe it.

After his talk ended, people crowded around him. I almost decided not to go. My mind kept saying, *“Why would he talk to you?”* But something pushed me forward. I introduced myself.

“Hi Sam, I’m Ravish. I’m a software engineer.”

He smiled and said, “Nice to meet you, Ravish.”

That simple moment already felt unreal.

I told him something I’ve been struggling with for months—that I feel confused. There are so many technologies: AI, web development, cloud computing, data science. I keep learning things but still feel like I’m not moving toward anything meaningful.

Sam listened carefully. Then he said something that struck me deeply:

“Don’t chase technologies. Chase important problems.”

I had never thought about it like that. I always focused on *what to learn next*. But he suggested focusing on *what problems are worth solving*.

He also told me something very practical:

“The best way to grow as an engineer is to build things.”

He said tutorials can only take you so far. Real learning happens when your code breaks, when users complain, when you try to improve something that already exists.

Another thing he mentioned was AI. According to him, AI will change almost every industry in the coming years. He suggested that even if someone is a web developer or backend engineer, understanding AI will be incredibly valuable.

But the advice that stayed with me the most was this:

“Start before you feel ready.”

He said most people wait too long because they want their ideas to be perfect. But almost every successful product starts messy and improves over time.

Before leaving, he gave one last thought that I can’t stop thinking about:

“Most people underestimate what they can do in ten years.”

Walking back home tonight, I felt something different. Not pressure. Not confusion. Just motivation.

Maybe I’ve been thinking too small.

Instead of just preparing for jobs or learning random frameworks, maybe I should try building something meaningful.

I don’t know where this path will lead yet.
But today, after talking to Sam Altman, I feel like I finally understand one thing:

The future belongs to people who **build**.
